#2 English around the world

English is a global language, but it sounds completely different depending on where you go! Let's explore some of the biggest differences between Australian English and United States English.

1. Pronunciation of the letter "R"

US English is generally "rhotic," meaning speakers clearly pronounce the "r" at the end of words (like "car" or "water"). Australian English is "non-rhotic," meaning the "r" sound at the end of words is usually dropped and replaced with an "ah" sound (so "car" sounds like "cah" and "water" sounds like "watah").

2. Vowel Sounds

Australians use very different vowel shifts compared to Americans. The most famous example is the long "A" sound. To an American ear, the Australian pronunciation of the words "day" or "mate" can sometimes sound closer to "die" or "mite".

3. Everyday Vocabulary

Even when speaking the same language, the vocabulary can be completely different! For example, Americans say "fries", "flip-flops", "candy", and "sweater". Australians call these same things "chips", "thongs", "lollies", and "jumpers".

4. Slang and Abbreviations

Australians are famous for shortening everyday words and adding an "-o" or "-ie" to the end. For example, "afternoon" becomes "arvo", "breakfast" becomes "brekkie", and "mosquito" becomes "mozzie". Americans usually stick to the full words.
